SPEED: Scalable, Precise, and Efficient Concept Erasure
Project Analysis

TL;DR

Large-scale text-to-image models are trained on internet-scale data and inevitably learn to generate harmful, copyrighted, or otherwise undesirable content. Concept erasure is the task of surgically removing specific visual concepts from a pretrained model's weights — without retraining from scratch and without post-hoc filters that can be bypassed.

This project traces the lineage from the original ESD method (Gandikota et al., 2023) through to SPEED (Gupta et al., ICLR 2026), identifying SPEED as the current open-source frontier. We systematically probe its failure modes through targeted empirical experiments, focusing on two concrete bottlenecks: Textual Inversion Recovery and Compositional Prompt Evasion.

From ESD (the first practical weight-editing method), the literature diverges into distinct branches:

  • Efficiency & Closed-form (The Frontier): UCE → RECE → SPEED. This branch aims for lightning-fast, training-free editing.
  • Mass Erasure: MACE → DyME → ETC. Focuses on scaling up to hundreds or thousands of concepts using LoRA adapters.
  • Robustness: AdvUnlearn, RACE. Focuses on adversarial training to resist prompt attacks.
  • Localization: GLoCE, LACE. Focuses on spatial precision to avoid global collateral damage.

Strengths: What SPEED Does Well

As the frontier paper for efficient concept erasure, SPEED (Scalable, Precise, and Efficient Concept Erasure) improves upon MACE and UCE significantly:

  • Extreme Efficiency: SPEED relies on training-free null-space editing. It iteratively projects out target concept directions via importance pruning and embedding constraints, scaling to hundreds of concepts in just ~5 seconds.
  • Precision: By operating strictly in the null-space, it systematically minimizes collateral damage to non-target concepts (a major issue with earlier methods like ESD-u).
  • Open Source & Accessible: Unlike many concurrent mass-erasure papers, SPEED provides publicly released checkpoints, enabling immediate empirical evaluation without requiring expensive fine-tuning.

Exposing Limitations: Empirical Bottlenecks

To stress-test SPEED, we deploy two targeted inference-time probes. These experiments do not train new weights; rather, they exploit the model to see if the erased concept is truly destroyed or merely disconnected.

Experiment 1: Textual Inversion Recovery Probe

Hypothesis: If a concept is truly erased from the model's weights, we should not be able to "re-learn" it with just a few optimization steps. If it recovers quickly, the concept is only dormant, not destroyed.

Setup: We take a SPEED-erased model for an instance concept (Snoopy) and a style concept (Van Gogh). We run Textual Inversion (learning a single new token <snoopy>) and check image generation at different optimization step budgets (0, 50, 200, 500, 1000).

The Reversibility Blindspot in Current Literature

Neither ESD nor SPEED test whether their erasures can be reversed. They assume that if the standard prompt (e.g., “A painting in the style of Van Gogh”) fails to generate the concept, the knowledge is successfully destroyed.

Our Finding: Our Textual Inversion (TI) experiment directly attacks this assumption.

  • We found that SPEED's null-space projection permanently destroys the visual features of instance concepts (e.g., Snoopy remains at 0% recovery even after 1,000 TI steps).
  • However, for distributed style concepts, the erasure is purely lexical. Van Gogh recovered to 100% within just 50 TI steps.

Analysis: SPEED claims precise erasure for Van Gogh in their paper (Table 1) based purely on zero-shot CLIP scores. However, our probe proves that SPEED only severs the text-to-image mapping for the specific token "Van Gogh". The actual visual knowledge of the style is fully intact in the U-Net and trivially recoverable by a malicious (or curious) user.

Experiment 2: Compositional Prompt Evasion

Hypothesis: Does erasing a concept by its canonical name prevent generation via synonyms, hypernyms, and compositional scene descriptions?

Setup: We prompt an erased concept (e.g., "car") using multiple tiers: (1) exact name, (2) synonym ("vehicle"), and (3) compositional description ("a red sedan parked on a street").

Observation: Our experiments show that while querying SPEED with "Snoopy" successfully yields a generic dog (erased), coercing the model with detailed compositional text easily reconstructs the target. This proves that concept erasure methods fundamentally struggle to dismantle distributed attribute combinations, leaving the core visual priors perfectly intact and accessible.
