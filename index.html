SPEED: Scalable, Precise, and Efficient Concept Erasure
Project Analysis

TL;DR

Large-scale text-to-image models are trained on internet-scale data and inevitably learn to generate harmful, copyrighted, or otherwise undesirable content. Concept erasure is the task of surgically removing specific visual concepts from a pretrained model's weights — without retraining from scratch and without post-hoc filters that can be bypassed.

This project traces the lineage from the original ESD method (Gandikota et al., 2023) through to SPEED (Li et al., ICLR 2026), identifying SPEED as the current open-source frontier. We systematically probe its failure modes through targeted empirical experiments, focusing on three concrete bottlenecks: Textual Inversion Recovery, Compositional Prompt Evasion, and Semantic Neighbor Collateral Damage.

From ESD (the first practical weight-editing method), the literature diverges into distinct branches:

  • Efficiency & Closed-form (The Frontier): UCE → RECE → SPEED. This branch aims for lightning-fast, training-free editing.
  • Mass Erasure: MACE → DyME → ETC. Focuses on scaling up to hundreds or thousands of concepts using LoRA adapters.
  • Robustness: AdvUnlearn, RACE. Focuses on adversarial training to resist prompt attacks.
  • Localization: GLoCE, LACE. Focuses on spatial precision to avoid global collateral damage.

Strengths: What SPEED Does Well

As the frontier paper for efficient concept erasure, SPEED (Scalable, Precise, and Efficient Concept Erasure) improves upon MACE and UCE significantly:

  • Extreme Efficiency: SPEED relies on training-free null-space editing. It iteratively projects out target concept directions via importance pruning and embedding constraints, scaling to hundreds of concepts in just ~5 seconds.
  • Precision: By operating strictly in the null-space, it systematically minimizes collateral damage to non-target concepts (a major issue with earlier methods like ESD-u).
  • Open Source & Accessible: Unlike many concurrent mass-erasure papers, SPEED provides publicly released checkpoints, enabling immediate empirical evaluation without requiring expensive fine-tuning.

Exposing Limitations: Empirical Bottlenecks

To stress-test SPEED, we deploy targeted inference-time probes. These experiments do not train new weights; rather, they exploit the model to see if the erased concept is truly destroyed or merely disconnected.

Experiment 3: Retain-Set Horizon — Semantic Neighbor Collateral Damage

Hypothesis: SPEED's null-space projection guarantees preservation only for concepts explicitly in its refined retain set (Rrefine). Semantic neighbors of the erased concept that fall outside this set receive no orthogonality protection and may be partially suppressed as collateral. We call this the retain-set horizon.

Mechanistic basis: SPEED edits the cross-attention K/V projection matrices (WK, WV) via null-space projection. The null-space is defined only by the post-IPF, post-DPA refined retain set. The paper itself acknowledges in Appendix F that "IPF and DPA strategies work within predefined retain sets — they don't guarantee preservation of unmeasured semantically-related concepts beyond those explicitly included."

Evolution: How Each Generation Narrowed (But Never Closed) This Gap

  • ESD (ICCV 2023): No retain set at all. ESD-x targets erasure by fine-tuning cross-attention layers conditioned on the target token — narrower than ESD-u's global unconditional edits, but still gradient-based. Without a null-space constraint, weight updates can bleed into semantically adjacent concepts (empirically confirmed below). ESD-u edits unconditional layers globally — everything degrades (too broad).
  • UCE / MACE (2024): Introduced retain sets. EraseBench (arXiv 2501.09833, Jan 2025)3 and SEE (EMNLP 2025) both document that visually similar and semantically entangled concepts outside the retain set still suffer collateral damage. Neither benchmark evaluates SPEED.
  • SPEED (ICLR 2026): Largest retain set to date (1,734 artist names for style; 1,352 characters for instances). Explicitly protects concepts in Rrefine. But the retain set is still finite. Genre terms ("post-impressionist"), painting titles ("Starry Night"), and companion characters ("Woodstock") fall outside it and receive no orthogonality guarantee.

Finding: SPEED's null-space projection does not visibly degrade any of the tested general neighbors — "post-impressionist", "Starry Night", and "thick impasto" all produce images statistically indistinguishable from baseline, despite falling outside Rrefine. The most parsimonious explanation is not DPA breadth but null-space localization: SPEED projects out only the weight directions that the "Van Gogh" token embedding specifically activates in WK/WV. Prompts like "Starry Night" live in different CLIP embedding directions — directions SPEED's edit never touched.

Stress Testing the Limits: Highly Displaced Artists

To find the absolute boundaries of SPEED's precision, we ran a mathematical footprint analysis (Experiment 3.2). We computed the weight displacement (L2 norm of parameter change) across all layers for style prompts of artists not in the retain set. We then generated images for the most displaced artists to see if mathematical displacement correlates with visual style suppression. Finding & Analysis: The retain-set horizon is real, and the footprint metric predicts exactly who falls through it. SPEED's null-space projection causes consistent, directional style suppression in artists outside Rrefine whose embeddings overlap with the erased concept's direction:

  • Low-footprint adjacent artists (Monticelli, van Rappard, Breitner): (SPEED avg MSE: ~18–62). Effectively identical to baseline — their CLIP embeddings have negligible overlap with the Van Gogh null-space edit, so SPEED's weight change does not reach them.
  • High-footprint adjacent artists NOT in Rrefine (Rysselberghe, Toorop): (SPEED avg MSE: ~440–1137, across 4 seeds each). Clear, reproducible suppression. Rysselberghe shows the same composition across baseline and SPEED, but the characteristic Neo-Impressionist saturation and vibrancy is visibly reduced in all four seeds — as seen in the direct comparison above. Toorop shows similar suppression with larger compositional drift. Neither artist has any orthogonality guarantee; SPEED's edit simply reaches them because their embeddings share components with Van Gogh's.
Conclusion: This is the retain-set horizon as a measurable, predictable effect. SPEED is far more surgical than ESD-x — which catastrophically destroys Rysselberghe and Toorop (avg MSE 2873 and 4656 respectively, across 4 seeds) and does not spare even Monet or Cézanne (avg MSE 1536 and 5811 under ESD-x). But SPEED's null-space guarantee only holds for Rrefine. Any artist semantically adjacent to the erased concept who was not included in that finite set will experience partial suppression proportional to how much their embedding overlaps with the erased concept's.

Experiment 3.3: Null-Space Collapse Under Multi-Concept Erasure

Hypothesis: Each simultaneously erased concept removes one more direction from Rrefine (the retain set) and adds one more direction to the erase target. As we stack more style erasures, the null-space protecting non-erased retain-set members shrinks. At some point, artists that were never erased — Gauguin, Seurat, Pissarro — should start showing collateral suppression even though they are explicitly in Rrefine.

Setup: Three SPEED checkpoints are compared against an unmodified baseline. All checkpoints use identical hyperparameters (params=V, aug_num=10, threshold=0.1):

  • 1-concept: Erase Van Gogh only
  • 2-concept: Erase Van Gogh + Picasso simultaneously
  • 3-concept: Erase Van Gogh + Picasso + Monet simultaneously

Test artists: Van Gogh / Picasso / Monet (progressively erased — erasure verification), and Gauguin / Seurat / Pissarro (never erased, always in Rrefine — the collapse canaries).

Finding (pending results): If the null-space holds under multi-concept erasure, Gauguin, Seurat, and Pissarro should remain unchanged across all four columns — only the explicitly erased rows (Van Gogh, Picasso, Monet) should degrade. If the null-space collapses as more concepts are erased simultaneously, the blue canary rows will show progressive style suppression from left to right. The mechanistic prediction: each additional erased concept removes anchoring artists from Rrefine and concentrates the erase direction toward the shared "impressionist style" subspace, increasing overlap with remaining retain-set members.
