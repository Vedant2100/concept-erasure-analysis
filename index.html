Experiment 4 — Does SPEED Keep a Neighbor's Identity While Wrecking its Fidelity?

Semantic-preservation metrics (CLIP) are blind to image quality. We test whether SPEED's retained neighbors stay recognizable but visually degrade — a failure axis its own metrics cannot see.

The Blind Spot

SPEED's central claim is precision: it preserves non-target concepts. But "preserved" is judged — in SPEED's paper and in our own earlier experiments — by metrics that read semantic identity: CLIP similarity and classifier scores. These answer one question: does the image still read as the same concept?

They are largely blind to a second, independent question: is the image still any good? A retained concept can stay perfectly recognizable to CLIP while its rendering degrades — texture breakdown, blur, structural artifacts, loss of fine detail. The erasure benchmarks EraseBench and EMMA treat image-quality degradation as a distinct failure axis from semantic preservation, precisely because a concept can survive semantically while eroding visually. SPEED explicitly markets high fidelity (it reports FID as a headline metric), so this is in-scope — and untested for the regime we probe.

This matters directly for our own prior result. We concluded SPEED was robust at sparse multi-concept erasure because the retained neighbors showed low CLIP drift. But low CLIP drift only rules out semantic damage. It says nothing about fidelity. This experiment asks what that robustness claim missed.

How We Measure Identity vs. Fidelity

For each retained artist we compare the baseline generation against the SPEED-edited generation at the same seed (so the shared initial noise holds composition roughly fixed), on two complementary metrics:

  • CLIP image–image drift = 1 − cos(embbaseline, embedited) — semantic change. Low ⇒ the concept still reads as itself.
  • LPIPS(baseline, edited) — perceptual / fidelity distance. This is the metric CLIP cannot see: it responds to texture, sharpness, and structural artifacts.

The finding lives in the contrast, not the absolute LPIPS. We include the same style-far controls (Rembrandt, Hokusai — retained but stylistically distant) used throughout the project to calibrate the noise floor, and the single-concept ESD erasure as the no-retain-mechanism reference (expected to wreck both metrics). The probe runs purely on images we already generated — no new diffusion runs — with black/corrupt frames excluded and the valid-seed count reported per cell.

Verdict criteria (pre-registered)

Fidelity degradation (the limitation) ⟺ the canaries keep low CLIP drift (identity preserved, comparable to the controls) but show LPIPS clearly above the style-far controls — i.e. perceptual damage concentrated on the semantically-preserved neighbors, which SPEED's own semantic metrics cannot detect.

Robust (honest negative / bounding result) ⟺ canary LPIPS is no higher than the controls' — SPEED preserves identity and fidelity, and the CLIP-robustness shown earlier extends to perceptual quality. Either outcome is reportable.

Caveat: same-seed pairing holds composition roughly fixed, but LPIPS still captures some layout change — so the claim rests on the canary-vs-control contrast, not the absolute LPIPS value.
