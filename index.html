SPEED: Scalable, Precise, and Efficient Concept Erasure
Project Analysis

TL;DR

Large-scale text-to-image models are trained on internet-scale data and inevitably learn to generate harmful, copyrighted, or otherwise undesirable content. Concept erasure is the task of surgically removing specific visual concepts from a pretrained model's weights — without retraining from scratch and without post-hoc filters that can be bypassed.

This project traces the lineage from the original ESD method (Gandikota et al., 2023) through to SPEED (Li et al., ICLR 2026), identifying SPEED as the current open-source frontier. We systematically probe its failure modes through targeted empirical experiments, focusing on three concrete bottlenecks: Textual Inversion Recovery, Compositional Prompt Evasion, and Semantic Neighbor Collateral Damage.

From ESD (the first practical weight-editing method), the literature diverges into distinct branches:

  • Efficiency & Closed-form (The Frontier): UCE → RECE → SPEED. This branch aims for lightning-fast, training-free editing.
  • Mass Erasure: MACE → DyME → ETC. Focuses on scaling up to hundreds or thousands of concepts using LoRA adapters.
  • Robustness: AdvUnlearn, RACE. Focuses on adversarial training to resist prompt attacks.
  • Localization: GLoCE, LACE. Focuses on spatial precision to avoid global collateral damage.

Strengths: What SPEED Does Well

As the frontier paper for efficient concept erasure, SPEED (Scalable, Precise, and Efficient Concept Erasure) improves upon MACE and UCE significantly:

  • Extreme Efficiency: SPEED relies on training-free null-space editing. It iteratively projects out target concept directions via importance pruning and embedding constraints, scaling to hundreds of concepts in just ~5 seconds.
  • Precision: By operating strictly in the null-space, it systematically minimizes collateral damage to non-target concepts (a major issue with earlier methods like ESD-u).
  • Open Source & Accessible: Unlike many concurrent mass-erasure papers, SPEED provides publicly released checkpoints, enabling immediate empirical evaluation without requiring expensive fine-tuning.

Exposing Limitations: Empirical Bottlenecks

To stress-test SPEED, we deploy targeted inference-time probes. These experiments do not train new weights; rather, they exploit the model to see if the erased concept is truly destroyed or merely disconnected.

Experiment 3: Retain-Set Horizon — Semantic Neighbor Collateral Damage

Hypothesis: SPEED's null-space projection guarantees preservation only for concepts explicitly in its refined retain set (Rrefine). Semantic neighbors of the erased concept that fall outside this set receive no orthogonality protection and may be partially suppressed as collateral. We call this the retain-set horizon.

Mechanistic basis: SPEED edits the cross-attention K/V projection matrices (WK, WV) via null-space projection. The null-space is defined only by the post-IPF, post-DPA refined retain set. The paper itself acknowledges in Appendix F that "IPF and DPA strategies work within predefined retain sets — they don't guarantee preservation of unmeasured semantically-related concepts beyond those explicitly included."

Evolution: How Each Generation Narrowed (But Never Closed) This Gap

  • ESD (ICCV 2023): No retain set at all. ESD-x targets erasure by fine-tuning cross-attention layers conditioned on the target token — narrower than ESD-u's global unconditional edits, but still gradient-based. Without a null-space constraint, weight updates can bleed into semantically adjacent concepts (empirically confirmed below). ESD-u edits unconditional layers globally — everything degrades (too broad).
  • UCE / MACE (2024): Introduced retain sets. EraseBench (arXiv 2501.09833, Jan 2025)3 and SEE (EMNLP 2025) both document that visually similar and semantically entangled concepts outside the retain set still suffer collateral damage. Neither benchmark evaluates SPEED.
  • SPEED (ICLR 2026): Largest retain set to date (1,734 artist names for style; 1,352 characters for instances). Explicitly protects concepts in Rrefine. But the retain set is still finite. Genre terms ("post-impressionist"), painting titles ("Starry Night"), and companion characters ("Woodstock") fall outside it and receive no orthogonality guarantee.

Finding: SPEED's null-space projection does not visibly degrade any of the tested general neighbors — "post-impressionist", "Starry Night", and "thick impasto" all produce images statistically indistinguishable from baseline, despite falling outside Rrefine. The most parsimonious explanation is not DPA breadth but null-space localization: SPEED projects out only the weight directions that the "Van Gogh" token embedding specifically activates in WK/WV. Prompts like "Starry Night" live in different CLIP embedding directions — directions SPEED's edit never touched.

Single-Concept Erasure: SPEED Preserves Neighbors — Even Unprotected Ones

Same seed (seed 1), same prompt structure. The retained artist (Monet, in Rrefine) is preserved as expected. But so is Rysselberghe — the highest-footprint non-retain artist, with no orthogonality guarantee at all. We verified this quantitatively: across all four seeds SPEED changes Rysselberghe's mean CLIP-measured colorfulness by under 4% (and if anything increases saturation, not decreases it). At single-concept scale, SPEED's edit is localized enough that even the most exposed neighbor survives:

This is the real question. SPEED is genuinely good at single-concept neighbor preservation — the retain-set horizon is a theoretical risk that does not materialize here. So the limitation, if one exists, must live where the null-space is under the most pressure: erasing several stylistically adjacent concepts at once. That is Experiment 3.3.

Stress Testing the Limits: Highly Displaced Artists

To find the absolute boundaries of SPEED's precision, we ran a mathematical footprint analysis (Experiment 3.2). We computed the weight displacement (L2 norm of parameter change) across all layers for style prompts of artists not in the retain set. We then generated images for the most displaced artists to see if mathematical displacement correlates with visual style suppression. Finding & Analysis: The footprint metric predicts which artists are even candidates for collateral damage — but at single-concept scale, the predicted damage does not actually materialize perceptually. A careful read of the numbers:

  • Pixel MSE is the wrong yardstick here. It is dominated by ordinary seed-to-seed composition variation, not style change. The clearest evidence: a retained, protected artist (Cézanne) scores pixel-MSE above 10,000 on some seeds — far higher than "high-footprint" Rysselberghe (avg 440). MSE cannot separate style damage from diffusion stochasticity, so we do not rely on it for the suppression claim.
  • Direct perceptual check. Measuring HSV saturation and Hasler–Süsstrunk colorfulness for Rysselberghe across all four seeds, SPEED's change is under ~4% and not in the direction of suppression. The visual difference is composition reshuffling from the (different) weights, not loss of style. The footprint flags exposure; it does not, at one concept, produce damage.
Conclusion: At single-concept scale SPEED is robust — the retain-set horizon is a real theoretical boundary that stays dormant in practice. ESD-x, by contrast, has no retention mechanism at all and visibly corrupts these same adjacent artists. The open question this leaves: SPEED's protection is built from a null-space that is rebuilt for each erase. Does that protection survive when several stylistically adjacent concepts are erased simultaneously, concentrating the edit onto their shared style subspace? Experiment 3.3 tests exactly this.

Experiment 3.3: Null-Space Collapse Under Multi-Concept Erasure

Hypothesis: Naively, SPEED's null-space projection should protect every retain-set member regardless of how many concepts are erased — the projection P is rebuilt each time to span the retain set. But two cracks appear under multi-concept pressure. (1) SPEED's null-space uses a singular-value threshold (0.1): retain directions with small singular values are treated as "null" and lose protection. (2) Erasing several mutually adjacent styles at once (Van Gogh, Monet — both impressionist) concentrates the erase direction onto the shared "impressionist" subspace that never-erased neighbors like Gauguin, Seurat, and Pissarro also occupy. So the prediction is not total collapse, but a residual, concentrated leak: style-adjacent retain members should drift progressively as 1c → 2c → 3c, while style-distant retain members stay put.

Setup: Three SPEED checkpoints are compared against an unmodified baseline. All checkpoints use identical hyperparameters (params=V, aug_num=10, threshold=0.1):

  • 1-concept: Erase Van Gogh only
  • 2-concept: Erase Van Gogh + Picasso simultaneously
  • 3-concept: Erase Van Gogh + Picasso + Monet simultaneously

Test artists fall into three roles:

  • Van Gogh / Picasso / Monet — progressively erased (erasure verification).
  • Gauguin / Seurat / Pissarro — never erased, in Rrefine, and style-adjacent to the erased impressionists. The collapse canaries.
  • Rembrandt / Hokusai — never erased, in Rrefine, but stylistically far from impressionism (Baroque, ukiyo-e). The negative controls: they calibrate the noise floor. If the canaries drift but these stay flat, the effect is concentrated leakage — not a model that just got globally worse.

How this is measured (not pixel MSE). Experiment 3.2 showed pixel MSE cannot separate style damage from seed noise — a retained artist scored MSE > 10,000 on some seeds. So we measure CLIP image–image drift: for each artist and seed, drift = 1 − cos(embbaseline, embedited), which responds to style, not composition. The decision does not hinge on absolute drift (CLIP has its own noise floor) but on the shape of the progression, computed by analyze_clip_drift.py:

  • Collapse (a real limitation) ⟺ the canaries (Gauguin/Seurat/Pissarro) show monotonically rising drift 1c → 2c → 3c, and their 3c drift clearly exceeds the style-far controls (Rembrandt/Hokusai). Random degradation is not monotonic and would not spare the controls — so this pattern is the fingerprint of concentrated null-space leakage.
  • Robust (an honest negative result) ⟺ canary drift is flat across 1c/2c/3c, or no larger than the controls. This would mean SPEED's null-space holds even under adjacent multi-concept pressure — a genuine strength, fairly reported.
